# 老虎的尾巴和狐狸的耳朵

要去意大利，于是看看Erwin Panofsky的***Renaissance and Renascences in Western Art*** (Westview Press, 1960)，还没进入核心内容，先看到两个有趣的比喻，于是记录下来。

那本书的内容是作者在某大学（我没听说过的，也就没记住）的演讲。他说，当他拿讲稿准备出版时，才发现……

I realized that I **had a tiger by the tail** or—to use the even more telling simile of Emperor Tiberius—"**a wolf by the ears**".



在这个语境下，两个比喻很容易理解。但是，如果两个短语单独出现，我肯定要误会“狐狸的耳朵”，不会想到它与“老虎尾巴”一样可怕（不过我们似乎更喜欢“老虎的屁股”）。我们还有“狐狸尾巴”呢，更与它风马牛不相及了。



我有些词典，难得一用，正好拿来“考证”一下。***NTC’s Dictionary of American Slang and Colloquial Expressions***（3rd edition）对“老虎尾巴”的解释是：

**to have become associated with something powerful and potentially dangerous. **

*You have a tiger by the tail. You bit off more than you could chew.*

*You’ve had a tiger by the tail ever since you took office.*



McGraw-Hill版的Idiom大典也是一样的解释（当然了，那个NTC似乎就是它的简本），从它还知道，tiger可以换成bear。这两本书都没收“狐狸耳朵”。据说，古罗马**Tiberius**说过，统治罗马就像“逮着狐狸的耳朵”，这句话在欧洲很流行，美国人不喜欢？



十分流行的***Oxford Dictionary of Idioms***的解释给出了十分令人欣喜的解释，而且将两个比喻相互参照：



**have a tiger by the tail ****have embarked on a course of action which proves unexpectedly difficult but which cannot easily or safely be abandoned. **

An alternative way of referring to the same predicament is *ride *a *tiger, *which alludes to the **Chinese saying***he who rides a tiger cannot dismount. *A similar difficulty confronts those who **have a wolf by the ears**.



**Have *(or *hold) a wolf by the ears ****be in a precarious position.**

The saying became current in English in j the mid 16th century, but the Roman comic dramatist Terence (195-159 BC) mentions its Latin equivalent, *lupum auribus tenere, *as already being an old saying in his time. 

**1990 **George **Will ***Suddenly *A Communist Party administering an economy is holding a wolf by the ears.



“骑虎难下”——洋人编写的英文词典里的中文俗语大概不会多，这是一个难得的例子。
