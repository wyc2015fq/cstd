在学习麦克斯韦方程组和光的波动方程之前，先看电磁波沿同轴电缆传播的例子，这会让你确信光就是电磁波的事实。

1.	一个电容上的电荷量是： ![Q = CV \Rightarrow {q_n} = C{V_n}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=Q+%3D+CV+%5CRightarrow+%7Bq_n%7D+%3D+C%7BV_n%7D) ；

3.	由电荷守恒可得 ![{I_n} - {I_{n + 1}} = \frac{{{\text{d}}{q_n}}}{{{\text{d}}t}}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7BI_n%7D+-+%7BI_%7Bn+%2B+1%7D%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7D%7Bq_n%7D%7D%7D%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dt%7D%7D) .

This is the desired wave equation, and it happens to be dispersionless.[1] 

We could derive a wave equation from analyzing the motion of a tiny piece of string too.

Everything there is to know about waves on a uniform string can be found by applying Newton’s Second Law, ![\vec F = m\vec a](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+m%5Cvec+a) , to one tiny bit of the string. Well, at least this is true of the small amplitude waves we shall be studying—we’ll be assuming the deviation of the string from its rest position is small compared with the wavelength of the waves being studied. This makes the math simpler, and is an excellent approximation for musical instruments, etc. Having said that, we’ll draw diagrams, like the one below, with rather large amplitude waves, to show more clearly what’s going on.
![](https://pic2.zhimg.com/v2-7f57d961e72cb346e3be83a0180ba891_b.jpg)
Let’s write down ![\vec F = m\vec a](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+m%5Cvec+a) for the small length of string between x and ![x + \Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=x+%2B+%5CDelta+x) in the diagram above. 

Taking the string to have mass density μ kg/m, we have ![m = \mu \Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=m+%3D+%5Cmu+%5CDelta+x) .

The forces on the bit of string (neglecting the tiny force of gravity, air resistance, etc.) are the tensions T at the two ends. The tension will be uniform in magnitude along the string, but the string curves if it’s waving, so the two ![\vec T](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+T) vectors at opposite ends of the bit of string do not quite cancel, this is the net force ![\vec F](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F) we’re looking for.

Bearing in mind that we’re only interested here in small amplitude waves, we can see from the diagram (squashing it mentally in the y-direction) that both ![\vec T](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+T) vectors will be close to horizontal, and, since they’re pointing in opposite directions, their sum—the net force ![\vec F](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F) —will be very close to vertical:
![](https://pic2.zhimg.com/v2-d84502402b0fe4069035cc53d6571ec9_b.jpg)
The vertical component of the tension ![\vec T](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+T) at the ![x + \Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=x+%2B+%5CDelta+x) end of the bit of string is ![T\sin \theta ](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=T%5Csin+%5Ctheta+) , where θ is the angle of slope of the string at that end. This slope is of course just ![{{\rm{d}}y\left( {x + \Delta x} \right)}/\rm{d} x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7B%7B%5Crm%7Bd%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+%5CDelta+x%7D+%5Cright%29%7D%2F%5Crm%7Bd%7D+x) , or, more precisely, ![{\text{d}}y/{\text{d}}x = \tan \theta ](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dy%2F%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dx+%3D+%5Ctan+%5Ctheta+)
![](https://pic1.zhimg.com/v2-79ca456cfd40702e66e86b0a984d06c4_b.jpg)
However, if the wave amplitude is small, as we’re assuming, then θ is small, and we can take ![\tan \theta  = \sin \theta  = \theta ](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Ctan+%5Ctheta++%3D+%5Csin+%5Ctheta++%3D+%5Ctheta+) , and therefore take the vertical component of the tension force on the string to be ![T\theta  = T{\text{d}}y\left( {x + \Delta x} \right)/{\text{d}}x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=T%5Ctheta++%3D+T%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+%5CDelta+x%7D+%5Cright%29%2F%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dx) ( ![y\left( {x + \Delta x} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=y%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+%5CDelta+x%7D+%5Cright%29) means ![f\left( {x + \Delta x} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=f%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+%5CDelta+x%7D+%5Cright%29) ).So the total vertical force from the tensions at the two ends becomes 

![\vec F = T\left( {\frac{{{\text{d}}y\left( {x + \Delta x} \right)}}{{{\text{d}}x}} - \frac{{{\text{d}}y\left( x \right)}}{{{\text{d}}x}}} \right) \cong T\frac{{{{\text{d}}^{\text{2}}}y\left( x \right)}}{{{\text{d}}{x^2}}}\Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+T%5Cleft%28+%7B%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+%5CDelta+x%7D+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dx%7D%7D+-+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+x+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dx%7D%7D%7D+%5Cright%29+%5Ccong+T%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7D%5E%7B%5Ctext%7B2%7D%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+x+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7D%7Bx%5E2%7D%7D%7D%5CDelta+x)

(导数与无穷小的关系)

the equality becoming exact in the limit ![\Delta x \to 0](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5CDelta+x+%5Cto+0) .

At this point, it is necessary to make clear that y is a function of t as well as of x: ![y = y\left( {x,t} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=y+%3D+y%5Cleft%28+%7Bx%2Ct%7D+%5Cright%29) . In this case, the standard convention for denoting differentiation with respect to one variable while the other is held constant (which is the case here—we’re looking at the sum of forces at one instant of time) is to replace ![{\text{d}}/{\text{d}}x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7D%2F%7B%5Ctext%7Bd%7D%7Dx) with ![\partial /\partial x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cpartial+%2F%5Cpartial+x)  .

So we should write:

![\vec F = T\frac{{{\partial ^{\text{2}}}y\left( x \right)}}{{\partial {x^2}}}\Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+T%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%5E%7B%5Ctext%7B2%7D%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+x+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%7Bx%5E2%7D%7D%7D%5CDelta+x)

The final piece of the puzzle is the acceleration of the bit of string: in our small amplitude approximation, it’s only moving up and down, that is, in the y-direction—so the acceleration is just ![{\partial ^2}y/\partial {t^2}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7B%5Cpartial+%5E2%7Dy%2F%5Cpartial+%7Bt%5E2%7D) , and canceling ![\Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5CDelta+x) between the mass ![m = \mu \Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=m+%3D+%5Cmu+%5CDelta+x) and ![\vec F = T\frac{{{\partial ^{\text{2}}}y\left( x \right)}}{{\partial {x^2}}}\Delta x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+T%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%5E%7B%5Ctext%7B2%7D%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+x+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%7Bx%5E2%7D%7D%7D%5CDelta+x) , ![\vec F = m\vec a](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+m%5Cvec+a) gives:

![T\frac{{{\partial ^{\text{2}}}y\left( x \right)}}{{\partial {x^2}}} = \mu \frac{{{\partial ^{\text{2}}}y}}{{\partial {t^2}}}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=T%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%5E%7B%5Ctext%7B2%7D%7D%7Dy%5Cleft%28+x+%5Cright%29%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%7Bx%5E2%7D%7D%7D+%3D+%5Cmu+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%5E%7B%5Ctext%7B2%7D%7D%7Dy%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+%7Bt%5E2%7D%7D%7D)

This is called the wave equation.

It’s worth looking at this equation to see why it is equivalent to ![\vec F = m\vec a](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+F+%3D+m%5Cvec+a) . Picture the graph ![y = y\left( {x,t} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=y+%3D+y%5Cleft%28+%7Bx%2Ct%7D+%5Cright%29) , showing the position of the string at the instant t. At the point x, the differential ![\partial y/\partial x](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cpartial+y%2F%5Cpartial+x) is the slope of the string. The second differential, ![{\partial ^2}y/\partial {x^2}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7B%5Cpartial+%5E2%7Dy%2F%5Cpartial+%7Bx%5E2%7D) , is the rate of change of the slope—in other words, how much the string is curved at x. And, it’s this curvature that ensures the ![\vec T](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cvec+T) ’s at the two ends of a bit of string are pointing along slightly different directions, and therefore don’t cancel. This force, then, gives the mass×acceleration vertically on the right.[? ]

**Solving the Wave Equation**

Now that we’ve derived a wave equation from analyzing the motion of a tiny piece of string, we must check to see that it is consistent with our previous assertions about waves, which were based on experiment and observation. For example, we stated that a wave traveling down a rope kept its shape, so we could write ![y\left( {x,t} \right) = f\left( {x - vt} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=y%5Cleft%28+%7Bx%2Ct%7D+%5Cright%29+%3D+f%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+-+vt%7D+%5Cright%29) . Does a general function ![f\left( {x - vt} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=f%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+-+vt%7D+%5Cright%29) necessarily satisfy the wave equation? This f is a function of a single variable, let’s call it ![u = x - vt](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=u+%3D+x+-+vt) . On putting it into the wave equation, we must use the chain rule for differentiation:

![\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial x}} = \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial u}}\frac{{\partial u}}{{\partial x}} = \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial u}},\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial t}} = \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial u}}\frac{{\partial u}}{{\partial t}} =  - v\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial u}}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+x%7D%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+x%7D%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D%2C%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+t%7D%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+t%7D%7D+%3D++-+v%5Cfrac%7B%7B%5Cpartial+f%7D%7D%7B%7B%5Cpartial+u%7D%7D)

so the function ![f\left( {x - vt} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=f%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+-+vt%7D+%5Cright%29) will always satisfy the wave equation provided ![{v^2} = \frac{T}{\mu }](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7Bv%5E2%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7BT%7D%7B%5Cmu+%7D)

All traveling waves move at the same speed—and the speed is determined by the tension and the mass per unit length. We could have figured out the equation for ![{v^2}](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=%7Bv%5E2%7D) dimensionally, but there would have been an overall arbitrary constant. We need the wave equation to prove that constant is 1.

Incorporating the above result, the equation is often written:

Of course, waves can travel both ways on a string: an arbitrary function ![f\left( {x + vt} \right)](https://www.zhihu.com/equation?tex=f%5Cleft%28+%7Bx+%2B+vt%7D+%5Cright%29) is an equally good solution.
